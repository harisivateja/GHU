Ethical & Social Considerations

Responsible use of predictive analytics for student success

Our Ethical Responsibility

The Centralized Student Dashboard involves managing sensitive student data and generating risk classifications that could significantly impact students' educational experiences. We recognize the substantial ethical responsibilities in protecting privacy, minimizing harm, and promoting equity throughout the system's design and deployment.

Core Ethical Principles

1. Privacy and Data Protection (FERPA Compliance)

The system is designed with strict adherence to the Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act (FERPA) principles:

  • Role-Based Access Control: Advisors can only view assigned students' records; department chairs and administrators access aggregated or department-level metrics
  • Data Minimization: Collection limited to information directly relevant for engagement monitoring and retention support
  • De-identification for Reporting: Student identifiers removed for external reporting; data fully anonymized for institutional analytics
  • Audit Trails: All data access logged for accountability and security monitoring
  • Secure Storage: Encrypted databases with restricted access and regular security audits

2. Algorithmic Bias and Fairness

The risk scoring model requires continuous evaluation to ensure it does not reinforce existing inequities:

  • Potential for Bias: High weighting on GPA and attendance may disadvantage students with chronic health conditions, family responsibilities, or disabilities requiring flexible participation
  • Financial Component Concerns: The 10% financial weighting may disproportionately identify low-income students as higher risk, which must be communicated as retention risk due to financial strain, not academic weakness
  • Contextual Interpretation Required: Risk scores cannot be definitive labels; advisors must explore individual student contexts before intervention
  • Regular Bias Audits: Systematic analysis across demographic groups to identify and address disparate impact
  • Model Transparency: Clear communication of algorithm factors and weighting to all stakeholders

3. Student Agency and Dignity

Students must maintain autonomy in their educational journey:

  • No Direct Score Disclosure: Students should not be informed of their numerical risk scores to avoid stigmatization and self-fulfilling prophecy effects
  • Support-Focused Outreach: All advisor interventions framed as resource offerings and encouragement, not punitive actions
  • Opt-Out Mechanisms: Students should have agency to decline non-mandatory support services without penalty
  • Positive Framing: Communications emphasize student strengths and available resources rather than deficiencies

4. Responsible Deployment and Oversight

Ongoing monitoring and governance are essential for ethical operation:

  • Ethics Review Board: Regular assessment by interdisciplinary committee including faculty, students, and privacy experts
  • Impact Evaluation: Measurement of intervention outcomes to ensure the system improves, not harms, student success
  • Continuous Model Refinement: Regular updates based on new research and institutional data to improve accuracy and fairness
  • Transparent Limitations: Clear communication that predictions are probabilities, not certainties, and can be wrong

Guiding Principles for Implementation

Beneficence

System designed to benefit students by enabling timely support and intervention

Non-Maleficence

Minimize potential harms including privacy violations, stigmatization, and bias

Autonomy

Respect student agency in educational decisions and support acceptance

Justice

Ensure equitable access to support services and fair algorithmic treatment

Transparency

Open communication about system operation, data use, and decision-making processes

Accountability

Clear responsibility structures for system governance, outcomes, and error correction
